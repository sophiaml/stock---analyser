P/E ratio
Price-to-Earnings ratio. Shows how much investors pay per $1 of company earnings. A P/E of 20 means you pay $20 for every $1 the company earns. Lower can mean cheaper, but context matters.
EPS
Earnings Per Share — how much profit the company makes per share. Example: EPS of $6.50 means the company earns $6.50 for each share outstanding. Find this on Yahoo Finance or Google Finance.
Sector
The industry the company operates in. Each sector has a different typical P/E ratio — tech stocks usually trade at higher P/Es than banks or energy companies.
52W high ($)
The highest price the stock reached in the last 52 weeks (1 year). Helps understand where the stock sits relative to its recent peak.
52W low ($)
The lowest price the stock reached in the last 52 weeks. If the stock is near its low, it might be a buying opportunity — or a warning sign. Context matters.
Dividend yield (%)
The annual cash payment the company gives shareholders, as a % of the stock price. Example: 2% dividend on a $100 stock = $2/year per share. Enter 0 if the stock pays no dividend.

Risk / Reward calculator: Before buying, always know your plan. Set a target price (take profit) and a max loss price (stop loss). The golden rule is a ratio of 2:1 or higher — meaning you aim to gain at least $2 for every $1 you risk.
Entry price ($)
The price at which you plan to buy the stock. This is your starting point for the calculation.
Capital ($)
How much money you want to invest in this trade. We'll calculate how many shares you can buy and your total potential gain or loss.
Take profit ($)
The target price where you plan to sell and take your profit. Set this based on technical resistance levels or your return target.
Stop loss ($)
The price where you cut your losses and sell if the stock goes against you. This protects you from bigger losses. Always set a stop loss before entering a trade.

Buy alert ($)
The price you want to be alerted at to consider buying. Usually set below the current price — when it dips to your target entry level.

The price at which you want to consider selling or taking profits. Usually above the current price.

A price level where the stock has historically bounced back up. If the stock falls to support and holds, it can be a buying opportunity.
Resistance ($)
A price level where the stock has struggled to break above. If it breaks through resistance, it can signal a strong upward move.
